A Sonnet for the Night

In the tongue of olden hours, a new heart speaks — a web-page as parchment, and words as candlelight.

Oh gentle night, whose silver fingers braid The sleeping meadow's breath in rivulets of dew, Thy hush, a chalice which the stars have laid Against the brows of moons that wander through.
When sorrow's bell tolls soft in distant lanes, And mortal cares sit heavy on the tongue, Thy cloak, unmarked by Time's impatient pains, Wraps tender souls till breaking of the sun.
Yet mark how Love, a pilgrim, treads thy floor, With footsteps measured by a secret rhyme; His whisper plucks the lyre of ages' store, And bends the stubborn lattice bars of time.
So let this night, in patient wisdom wreathed, Hold fast the dreams which daylight loosens free; For every sorrow 'fore the dawn bequeathed Shall find in morning's mouth its melody. — Rendered in the manner of the antique stage.
